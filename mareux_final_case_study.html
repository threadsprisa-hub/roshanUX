Case Study — Fashion E-Commerce

MAREUX

MAREUX is a premium minimal fashion e-commerce concept designed for the intentional wardrobe. The project involved designing a complete shopping experience — from landing pages and product discovery to product detail pages, seasonal gift kits, and a handwritten note gifting flow. Built around a warm editorial aesthetic, the experience uses refined typography, subtle border systems, and carefully considered interactions to create a premium feel without relying on dark themes or excessive decoration.

Early Observations

  • The interface feels visually crowded, making it hard to focus on primary actions.
  • Product information appears scattered rather than grouped logically.
  • Color usage feels overwhelming and competes with content instead of supporting it.
  • Typography sizes and weights feel inconsistent across sections.
  • Interaction feedback is unclear, especially when users select options like color or size.

Objective / Goal

Design a fashion e-commerce experience that actually feels like the brand it represents — not a template with a logo dropped in. MAREUX required building every screen, every flow, and every interaction from first principles, where the design itself becomes the brand identity.

My Design Process

Research

Competitive Market Analysis

I conducted a competitive market analysis of clothing brands such as HUBA and Phasezero, both of which feature clean, minimal interfaces and highly efficient user experiences. My analysis focused on how these modern design approaches influence the user experience, particularly for a younger, tech-savvy audience. I observed that their intuitive navigation and streamlined interactions not only enhance usability but also have a direct impact on customer engagement and, ultimately, sales performance.

User Interviews

To gain a better understanding of user needs, I conducted user interviews

I conducted a heuristic evaluation of KTM City's existing website, analysing the interface against established UX principles. Based on my observations of the live product, I identified patterns of friction that commonly affect users in similar e-commerce contexts.

Mapping Out User Flows

Outdated Local UX Patterns

Many Nepali clothing websites rely on loud visuals and information dumping rather than clarity, structure, and calm decision-making. This creates friction for modern users who expect cleaner, more intentional experiences.

Unclear Action Feedback

Users receive weak or no visual confirmation after performing actions such as selecting a color, adding an item to cart, or updating quantities. This creates doubt about whether the action was successful.

Low Purchase Confidence

Users struggle to make confident purchases because product information is scattered and poorly structured. Unclear sizing, inconsistent details, and weak visual hierarchy force guesswork instead of informed decisions.

Mapping Out User Flows

I sketched two user flows to visualize how users navigate the existing website and the redesigned experience. The original flow highlighted friction caused by the clustered layout, while the redesigned flow creates a more intentional and natural navigation path, reducing ambiguity and cognitive load.

Validated Problems

  • Cognitive overload due to a clustered interface.
  • Weak visual hierarchy in product listings, affecting scannability.
  • Scattered product details on product detail pages.
  • Inconsistent typography and layouts, reducing clarity.
  • Unclear system feedback for user actions.

Design Considerations

  • Reducing visual noise without removing essential business content.
  • Creating clear hierarchy while allowing scalability for future products.
  • Providing clear interaction feedback without relying on heavy animations.
  • Improving usability without alienating users used to visually loud interfaces.

High-Fidelity Design

I redesigned the website by first creating low-fidelity wireframes, which were then refined into mid- and high-fidelity versions. Building on these wireframes, I developed a high-fidelity design that brings the proposed interface to life with visual clarity and improved user experience.

Usability Testing

Lightweight usability testing was conducted to validate key user flows and interaction clarity.

Test Scenarios

  • Browse a product from the listing page.
  • Select size and color options.
  • Add the product to cart.
  • Access the cart and proceed toward checkout.
  • Add and view items in the wishlist.

Usability Test 1 — Findings

01

Accessing cart from the product listing page caused initial confusion.

02

Size selection lacked clear confirmation that the choice had been registered.

03

Wishlist icon was not immediately recognizable to all participants.

04

The need to scroll past promotional content before reaching product actions was friction-causing.

Usability Test 2 — Findings

"The layout feels calm and easy to understand." — Users appreciated the clean structure compared to typical Nepali e-commerce sites.

"I didn't have to search for basic information." — Clear grouping of price, size, and actions reduced effort while browsing.

"I expected shipping and return info here." — Users wanted delivery and return details directly on the product page.

Final Product

In the second round of testing, users encountered fewer and less severe usability issues, indicating that the design iterations successfully addressed the core problems. With two rounds of testing completed, final refinements were made to improve interaction feedback, visual clarity, and overall ease of use.

  • Identified missing trust information (shipping and returns) as a priority for future iterations.
  • Strengthened interaction feedback for add-to-cart and wishlist actions to improve system clarity.
  • Refined product detail hierarchy to surface sizing, price, and primary actions more clearly.

Project Takeaways

1) Solving small problems can have big implications

Through this project, I learned just how essential user research is in shaping effective and intuitive interfaces. By simulating interviews, mapping user flows, and analyzing usability issues, I was able to uncover patterns in user behavior that wouldn't have been obvious from the interface alone. This process highlighted the gap between assumptions and actual user needs, showing me that skipping research often leads to designs that look good but fail in usability.

2) Make the user's life obviously easy

This project reinforced that every design choice must be rooted in a deep understanding of the user. Conducting research, simulating interviews, and mapping detailed user flows allowed me to uncover the real pain points that users face when interacting with the website. Iterating on wireframes based on these insights taught me the value of testing multiple approaches before settling on a solution.

3) Fast iteration and validation promotes design learning

Through this project, I realized that even small details — like button states, hover effects, spacing, and visual feedback — play a huge role in making the interface feel intuitive and responsive. These micro-interactions help guide users, confirm their actions, and reduce friction, making the experience feel seamless and polished rather than confusing or frustrating.
